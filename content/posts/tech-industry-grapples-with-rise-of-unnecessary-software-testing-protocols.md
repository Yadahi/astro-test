Software engineers at several major technology companies are pushing back against what they describe as excessive and counterproductive testing mandates, arguing that poorly designed quality-assurance processes are costing firms millions of dollars in lost productivity annually.

A survey of 1,200 developers published Friday by the fictional Institute for Software Efficiency found that 67 percent of respondents said their organizations require tests that duplicate existing checks, contradict one another, or test conditions that cannot occur in production environments.

"We have automated tests that test the tests," said one senior engineer at a large software firm, who spoke on condition of anonymity because they were not authorized to speak publicly. "At some point it stops being quality assurance and starts being performance theater."

## A Growing Chorus of Complaints

The report identified several patterns critics call "testing nonsense" — a term that has gained traction in developer forums and online communities. These include writing unit tests for randomly generated data with no deterministic outcome, requiring 100 percent code coverage on auto-generated boilerplate files, and mandating integration tests for features that are never deployed to users.

According to the institute's data, the average developer at a mid-size tech company spends 11 hours per week writing or maintaining tests, compared with 7 hours five years ago. The report estimates this shift costs the industry approximately $4.2 billion annually in reduced feature output.

Not everyone agrees the trend is harmful. Quality assurance professionals argue that rigorous testing prevents costly outages and security breaches that can far exceed the overhead of writing the tests themselves.

"One production incident can wipe out months of development savings," said Jordan Ellis, a QA architect at a software consultancy. "The developers complaining today are often the same ones who complained last year when something broke in production."

## Industry Response

Several firms have begun auditing their testing suites to remove redundant or low-value checks. Engineers at two companies told reporters their teams reduced total test counts by more than 40 percent without any measurable increase in defects reaching users.

The institute recommended that engineering organizations adopt a tiered testing strategy, prioritizing tests that cover user-facing functionality and high-risk code paths over exhaustive but low-signal coverage metrics.

The full report is available on the institute's website. A follow-up study examining testing practices in regulated industries, including finance and healthcare, is expected later this year.
